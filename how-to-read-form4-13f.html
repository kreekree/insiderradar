Field Guide

How to Read SEC Form 4 and 13F Filings

The two filings hedge fund analysts read every morning — and what every field actually means. No jargon. No paywall.

Part 1: SEC Form 4 — Insider Trading Disclosures

What is Form 4?

Form 4 is a mandatory SEC disclosure that corporate insiders — including officers (CEO, CFO, COO), directors, and any shareholder owning more than 10% of a company's stock — must file within two business days of any transaction involving their company's securities. It is one of the most closely watched public filings in the investment community because it provides near-real-time visibility into whether the people with the most information about a company are buying or selling its stock.

Form 4 is governed by Section 16(a) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and has been publicly available on SEC EDGAR since 1996. You can search any company and view every insider transaction going back decades at no cost.

Key Fields in a Form 4

Field What It Means
Reporting Person The insider's name and their relationship to the company (officer, director, 10% owner)
Issuer The company whose securities were traded, identified by name and CIK number
Transaction Date The date the trade actually occurred (not the filing date)
Transaction Code A single letter indicating the transaction type (see table below)
Amount (Shares) Number of shares involved in the transaction
Price Per Share The price paid or received per share (may be $0 for awards)
Ownership After Total shares owned by this insider after the transaction closes

Transaction Codes — What Each Letter Means

Not all Form 4 transactions are meaningful. The transaction code is the single most important field to check first.

P — Open Market Purchase

The most bullish signal. The insider spent their own cash to buy shares at market price. This is a clear vote of confidence.

S — Open Market Sale

The insider sold shares for cash. Can be bearish, but often just reflects personal financial planning — diversification, home purchase, taxes.

A — Grant / Award

Shares or options awarded as compensation. Not a market signal — the company gave these to the insider, not a purchase.

M — Option Exercise

The insider exercised stock options to acquire shares. Often followed by an S (sale) to cover taxes. Look at the net position change.

F — Tax Withholding

Shares surrendered back to the company to cover tax obligations on a vesting event. Not a discretionary sale — ignore as a signal.

G / I — Gift / Inheritance

Shares gifted to charity or inherited from an estate. No market signal — the insider did not choose to buy or sell at market price.

⚠ Key Insight

The only Form 4 transactions worth treating as genuine buy/sell signals are code P (open-market purchase) and code S (open-market sale). Everything else — awards, option exercises, tax withholdings — is a contractual or administrative event, not a discretionary market decision.

How to Interpret Insider Buying

Academic research consistently shows that clusters of insider buying — multiple executives purchasing shares within a short window — is a stronger signal than a single large purchase. A CEO buying alone might reflect personal conviction; a CEO, CFO, and two board members all buying within the same week is harder to dismiss. Look for:

  • Multiple insiders buying within 30 days of each other
  • Purchase amounts that are large relative to the insider's salary or prior purchases
  • Buys occurring after a significant share price decline
  • Purchases by high-ranking officers (CEO, CFO) vs. directors who may receive advisory fees in stock

How to Interpret Insider Selling

Selling is harder to interpret because insiders sell for many reasons unrelated to company outlook — paying taxes, diversifying wealth, buying a house, funding a divorce. However, large coordinated selling by multiple insiders at elevated stock prices, especially following a strong run-up, can be a warning signal. Also watch for insiders who sell under a 10b5-1 plan (a pre-scheduled selling program) — these are set up months in advance and carry less signal than discretionary sales.

Part 2: SEC Form 13F — Institutional Holdings

What is a 13F?

Form 13F-HR is a quarterly disclosure required under Section 13(f) of the Securities Exchange Act for any institutional investment manager controlling $100 million or more in qualifying US equity securities. The filing must be submitted within 45 days of the end of each calendar quarter (March 31, June 30, September 30, December 31).

13F filings are the primary tool Wall Street uses to track hedge fund positioning. They reveal top holdings, position sizes in shares and dollar value, and whether positions are held outright or through options. Because filings are quarterly and up to 45 days delayed, the data is more a historical snapshot than a live feed — but it remains one of the only windows into how the world's most sophisticated funds are allocating capital.

Key Fields in a 13F InfoTable

Field What It Means
Name of Issuer The company whose stock is held (e.g. "APPLE INC")
Title of Class The specific security type, usually "COM" for common stock
CUSIP A 9-character identifier unique to each security, used to match positions across filings
Value ($000s) Fair market value in thousands of dollars as of the quarter-end date
Shares/PRN Amount Number of shares (or principal amount for debt securities) held
Put/Call Indicates whether the position is an options contract (Put or Call) rather than direct stock ownership
Investment Discretion SOLE = manager controls the position independently; SHARED = co-managed with another party

Important Limitations of 13F Data

  • No short positions — 13F only shows long equity holdings. A fund could be net short a stock while still reporting a small long position in the 13F.
  • Up to 45 days stale — by the time you see a 13F, the fund may have exited the position entirely.
  • No non-US securities — positions in foreign stocks, bonds, commodities, or crypto are not disclosed.
  • Confidential treatment — funds can apply to the SEC to keep sensitive positions hidden for a limited period if disclosure would harm their strategy.
  • $100M threshold — smaller managers are not required to file, so the data skews toward large institutional investors.

Best practice: Combine Form 4 insider trading data with 13F institutional positioning to build a fuller picture. If insiders are buying and hedge funds are increasing their positions in the same stock, that is a notably strong confluence signal.
