What Is SEC Form 4? A Retail Trader's Complete Guide to Insider Trading Disclosures

Every CEO, CFO, and board member is legally required to tell the public within 48 hours when they buy or sell their own company's stock. That filing is called Form 4 — and it's been sitting on a government website for free since 1996. Most retail investors have never heard of it. Here's everything you need to know.

By InsiderRadar · April 10, 2026 · 8 min read

The Information Asymmetry Nobody Talks About

There's a reason hedge funds pay analysts $250,000 a year to monitor SEC filings. Corporate insiders — the people running the company — have access to information that no earnings call, analyst note, or Reddit thread can replicate. They know the pipeline. They know the quarterly numbers before the quarterly numbers are public. They know whether the deal is actually going to close.

And when they choose to put their own money into the stock at market price, that's not a guess. That's a statement.

The SEC understood this in 1934 when it wrote Section 16(a) of the Securities Exchange Act. It created a mandatory disclosure system: any insider transaction — buy, sell, award, exercise — must be reported publicly within two business days. The filing is called Form 4. And for most of its existence, you had to dig through the SEC's EDGAR archive yourself to find it. Now, InsiderRadar does that work automatically.

Who Has to File Form 4?

Not every person connected to a public company is required to file Form 4. The disclosure requirement applies to Section 16 insiders, defined as:

  • ● Officers — CEO, CFO, COO, President, General Counsel, Chief Accounting Officer, and any officer whose title the company designates as subject to Section 16
  • ● Directors — Every member of the board of directors, regardless of whether they hold an executive role
  • ● 10%+ shareholders — Any person or entity that beneficially owns more than 10% of any class of a company's equity securities

Rank-and-file employees — engineers, salespeople, middle managers — are not subject to Form 4 requirements unless they also hold an officer title or sit on the board. This is important to understand: Form 4 data reflects decisions made by the most informed people in the company, not just anyone with access to a company brokerage account.

The 48-Hour Rule

Before 2002, insiders had until the end of the calendar month following the transaction — sometimes 40+ days — to disclose a trade. That window was closed by the Sarbanes-Oxley Act: insiders now must file Form 4 within two business days of any transaction. Miss the deadline and the SEC can investigate. The system is now close to real-time.

In practice, most filings appear on SEC EDGAR within 24–48 hours of the actual trade. Our insider trading tracker fetches and displays these filings automatically, so you see the same data as institutional compliance desks — often before financial media covers it.

Transaction Codes: The Only Field That Actually Matters First

Before you look at share count or dollar value, look at the transaction code. This single letter tells you what actually happened. Misreading it is the most common way investors misinterpret Form 4 data and draw the wrong conclusions.

P Open-Market Purchase — The Signal You're Looking For

The insider spent real cash to buy shares at market price. Nobody made them do this. They looked at the current stock price and decided it was worth their own money. This is the strongest Form 4 signal in existence. When you see code P, pay attention.

S Open-Market Sale — Context Required

The insider sold shares for cash. This is bearish in the same way a "P" is bullish — but with a crucial caveat: people sell for a thousand reasons unrelated to company conviction. Paying taxes, buying a house, diversifying an overconcentrated position, following a 10b5-1 plan set up months ago. A single S code is not alarming. A wave of S codes from multiple insiders simultaneously is worth watching.

A Award / Grant — Ignore as a Signal

The company gave shares to the executive as compensation. The insider paid nothing. This is an accounting event, not a market decision. Seeing a CEO receive $5M in stock awards tells you nothing about their view of the stock — that's just how executive comp works.

M Option Exercise — Look at What Comes Next

The insider converted stock options into actual shares. The exercise itself isn't the signal — look for an S transaction immediately after. If they exercise and hold, that's mildly bullish. If they exercise and immediately sell everything (a "same-day sale"), that's a routine tax move. The net share count change is what matters.

F Tax Withholding — Completely Automatic, Not a Signal

When restricted stock units (RSUs) vest, the company automatically withholds a portion of shares to cover the tax liability. This looks like a large "sale" on the filing but the insider had zero say in it. Code F is the single most misread transaction code by retail investors who see large insider "sells" that are actually just payroll tax administration.

What a Meaningful Signal Actually Looks Like

A single Form 4 purchase is a data point. A pattern is a signal. Here's what separates noise from something worth tracking:

  • Multiple insiders buying within the same 30-day window When a CEO buys alone, it might be a personal tax strategy or diversification. When the CEO, CFO, and two board members all independently file P transactions within three weeks, that's a statement. These people don't coordinate — they all just looked at the stock and decided it was cheap.
  • Purchases following a significant price decline An insider buying stock that has dropped 30–40% from its high is putting money where their mouth is. They know whether the decline is panic-driven or fundamental — and if they're buying, they've made that assessment.
  • Large purchases relative to the insider's position and salary A director buying $20,000 in stock is routine. A director buying $2 million — several times their annual board compensation — is a conviction bet. Check the "ownership after" field: if they're significantly increasing their total stake, pay attention.
  • Purchases by operating executives rather than outside directors CEOs, CFOs, and COOs have more granular knowledge of the business than outside directors. A C-suite purchase carries more informational weight than a director purchase, all else equal.

What Form 4 Can't Tell You

Form 4 is a tool, not an oracle. Know its limits:

  • It cannot tell you why an insider bought — only that they did, and for how much
  • It does not cover open-market transactions in options or derivatives (only stock)
  • Insiders can set up pre-scheduled selling plans (10b5-1) months in advance, which means an S filing might reflect a decision made long before current market conditions
  • The 48-hour window is still 48 hours — by the time a filing hits EDGAR, the stock may have already moved
  • Not all insiders are equally informed — a board member's purchase carries different weight than a CEO's
