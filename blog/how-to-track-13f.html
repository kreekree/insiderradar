How to Track Hedge Fund Positions Using Free SEC 13F Filings

Renaissance Technologies. Bridgewater. Citadel. These funds spend millions on research to identify the best stocks to own. They're also legally required to disclose those stocks to the public every three months. Here's how to read those disclosures — and exactly what they can and cannot tell you.

By InsiderRadar · April 10, 2026 · 10 min read

The Law That Forces Hedge Funds to Show Their Cards

In 1975, Congress passed an amendment to the Securities Exchange Act that required any institutional investment manager controlling more than $100 million in qualifying US equity securities to file a quarterly report disclosing their holdings. The form is called 13F-HR (Holdings Report), and it has been publicly available on the SEC's EDGAR database since 1993.

What this means in practice: Berkshire Hathaway, Renaissance Technologies, Citadel Advisors, Soros Fund Management, and thousands of other institutions must file a document every quarter telling the world exactly which stocks they own and how many shares. The SEC publishes it for free. No subscription required.

Most retail investors have no idea this data exists. The ones who do often use it wrong. This guide will walk you through how to find 13F filings, what each field means, and — critically — how to interpret the data without falling into the traps that catch most people.

Who Files 13F? The $100 Million Threshold

The 13F requirement applies to any institutional investment manager that exercises investment discretion over $100 million or more in Section 13(f) securities — which includes US exchange-listed stocks, ETFs, closed-end funds, and certain options.

In practice, this captures a massive range of institutions:

  • ●Hedge funds (the ones large enough to hit the $100M threshold)
  • ●Registered investment advisers (RIAs) managing client portfolios
  • ●Mutual fund companies
  • ●Pension funds and endowments
  • ●Family offices
  • ●Banks and insurance companies with equity investment arms

One important nuance: a firm can be a 13F filer without managing a hedge fund. Many 13F filers are straightforward long-only managers with no particular edge. When hunting for meaningful signals, focus on known active managers and concentrated funds where portfolio decisions reflect genuine conviction, not passive index replication.

The 13F Filing Timeline

13F filings are due within 45 calendar days after the end of each calendar quarter. That means:

The delay is the single most important limitation to internalize. By the time you read the latest 13F, you're seeing a portfolio snapshot from at least 45 days ago — and potentially from as far back as 135 days ago (45 days after a quarter that ended 90 days after the prior filing). A fund could have exited a position entirely and you wouldn't know until next quarter.

How to Read a 13F: The InfoTable

A 13F filing consists of two documents: a cover page and the InfoTable — a structured XML file listing every position. Each row in the InfoTable is one position. Here's what each column means:

Name of Issuer

The company name. May be abbreviated or use legal names that differ from the common name (e.g., "META PLATFORMS INC" instead of "Meta"). Use the CUSIP to confirm identity across filings.

CUSIP

A 9-character alphanumeric identifier unique to each security. This is the most reliable way to match a position across different 13F filings — company names and ticker symbols can change, but CUSIPs are stable. Track CUSIP-level changes between quarters to see additions, exits, and size changes.

Value (thousands)

Fair market value of the position as of the quarter-end date, reported in thousands of dollars. A value of "1,234,567" means $1.234 billion. This is the most useful field for understanding position size and portfolio weight. InsiderRadar automatically multiplies by 1,000 to show the actual dollar value.

Shares / PRN Amount

The number of shares held. Comparing this number between consecutive 13F filings tells you whether a fund added to, reduced, or held a position. A growing share count despite a stable or declining stock price indicates high conviction — the fund is buying more as the price falls.

Investment Discretion (SOLE / SHARED)

SOLE means the manager makes all decisions about this position independently. SHARED means it is co-managed with another entity. Focus on SOLE positions — SHARED positions may represent assets under advisory arrangements where the manager doesn't have full conviction or control.

Put / Call

If present, this column indicates the position is an options contract rather than a direct stock holding. A "CALL" position represents the right to buy shares (bullish). A "PUT" represents the right to sell (often used as a hedge, not necessarily bearish on the underlying). Most 13F holdings are direct stock positions with no Put/Call designation.

The Right Way to Use 13F Data

Given the inherent delay, 13F data is most useful for understanding a fund's long-term conviction and investment thesis rather than for timing trades. Here's how institutional analysts actually use this data:

1. Track Changes Quarter-Over-Quarter

The raw holdings are less useful than the delta. What did the fund add? What did it exit? What has it held for 8 consecutive quarters without reducing? Consistent multi-quarter additions to a position suggest the fund is building a conviction thesis, not chasing momentum.

2. Focus on Concentration

A position that represents 15% of a fund's entire portfolio is not the same as a position that represents 0.3%. The former is a high-conviction bet the manager has thought deeply about. The latter is a diversification holding. Always calculate position weight as a percentage of total reported AUM — InsiderRadar does this automatically in the Fund Tracker.

3. Cross-Reference With Form 4

13F tells you what institutions own. Form 4 tells you what insiders are doing with their own money. When both point in the same direction — institutions increasing positions while insiders are simultaneously buying on the open market — the confluence is a notably stronger signal than either alone.

4. Identify New Positions From High-Signal Filers

When a fund known for its research depth (think: a concentrated value fund, an activist, or a multi-year track record manager) establishes a brand-new position, that's more informative than a slight percentage increase in an existing holding. New entries into a portfolio represent a fresh underwriting decision — someone looked at the company and decided it deserved capital for the first time.

Critical Limitations: What 13F Won't Tell You

⚠ Read This Before Acting on Any 13F

13F filings reveal only long equity positions in US-listed securities. They are a partial picture at best. A fund with $10B in 13F holdings could also have $5B in short positions, $3B in non-US equities, and $2B in credit instruments — none of which appears in the 13F.

  • ✗ No short positions — The most famous hedge fund strategy of all time (short selling) is entirely absent from 13F data. A manager could be massively net short a stock they also show as a long position (a pairs trade or hedge).
  • ✗ No non-US securities — Foreign stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies, crypto — none of it. A global macro fund's 13F might represent only 20% of its actual risk exposure.
  • ✗ Up to 135 days stale — By the time you see it, the trade is ancient history. Momentum strategies, short-term trades, and any position entered or exited within the quarter are invisible.
  • ✗ Confidential treatment requests — Funds can apply to the SEC to delay disclosure of sensitive positions (typically activist stakes being built). The SEC often grants these for limited periods, meaning the largest, most market-moving positions may be the ones you don't see.
  • ✗ The $100M floor — Smaller, potentially higher-performing managers are not required to file. The 13F universe skews toward large, established institutions.

The Honest Verdict on "Following" Hedge Funds

13F data is valuable. It is not a trading strategy on its own.

The funds worth tracking — concentrated, high-conviction, long-term oriented — tend to build positions over many quarters. By the time you see their first 13F disclosure in a stock, they may have been accumulating for 6 months. That's also when the thesis is most likely to be mature and well-researched. Following a fund's highest-conviction multi-quarter positions has historically been more predictive than following new entries.

Use 13F data the way professionals use it: as a lens for identifying what the most patient, research-intensive capital in the world has conviction in — then do your own analysis to decide whether you agree. The smart money is not always right. But knowing where they've placed their bets is undeniably useful information to have.
